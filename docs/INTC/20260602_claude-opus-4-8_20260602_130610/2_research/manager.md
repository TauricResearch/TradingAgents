**Recommendation**: Underweight

**Rationale**: Alright, let me walk you through where I land on INTC after sitting through this whole debate, because both sides fought hard and there's real signal in the noise.

The bull's strongest, undisputed points are these: three consecutive quarters of positive operating income ($858M, $550M, $934M), a genuine gross margin recovery from a crisis-level 27.5% up to ~39.4%, and a $13.4B net debt reduction in twelve months. Those are facts, not narrative, and I'm not going to pretend the business isn't structurally healthier than it was 18 months ago. The bull also made a fair point on the strategic/geopolitical optionality — Intel really is the only U.S.-domiciled advanced-node IDM, and in a deglobalizing world that carries some premium that won't show up in a DCF.

But here's what tipped me toward the bear side. The single most damaging moment in the entire debate was when the bull built their own scenario table and conceded — in their own words — that "INTC at $109.33 is not a perfect entry point on pure EPS math," with earnings-based targets topping out around $101 even in the optimistic case. When the bull's own arithmetic puts fair value below the current price, that's a tell. The bull then retreated to "transitional multiples" and the Nvidia-2020 analogy, but the bear dismantled that cleanly: 2020 Nvidia already had 55%+ margins, positive FCF, CUDA's ecosystem moat, and dominant share. Intel today has 39.4% margins, -$2.54B quarterly FCF that's RE-accelerating (Q1 2026 CapEx came in HIGHER than Q4 2025, not lower), declining server share, and no AI software moat. The analogy doesn't hold.

The other arguments where the bear simply had better evidence: (1) Revenue has been flat at ~$52-54B for three years through the largest AI capex cycle in history while AMD and Nvidia grew dramatically — that's running to stand still, and the bull's "DCAI stabilization = AI revenue capture" claim was inference dressed as evidence, equally explainable by Xeon price cuts to defend share. (2) The $1.55 forward EPS underpinning the 69x multiple requires a near-tripling from the current ~$0.45 operating run-rate — achievable only if everything goes right simultaneously on a cost base already cut to the bone. (3) Three years of operating-leverage data show the leverage works violently against Intel on the downside, undercutting the bull's promise that it flips powerfully positive. (4) Every short-term technical indicator is pointing lower — price below 10 EMA and Bollinger middle, MACD histogram negative and deepening, distribution candles.

To be fair to the bull, the bear overreached in places — the original 0.85x interest coverage figure was an arithmetic error (it's really ~3.4x), the goodwill "6 years" framing was sloppy (it's ~6 quarters), and net debt reduction plus the margin trajectory are genuinely impressive. So this isn't a slam-dunk Sell. The business is improving, the balance sheet is healing, and a SELL/exit call assumes a complete reversal of demonstrated progress, which the data doesn't support.

Where I land: the asymmetry is unfavorable at $109.33. You're paying a full-dressed price for a turnaround that's real but unproven, with negative FCF, a stretched multiple, identifiable competitive threats (Nvidia RTX Spark into the high-margin PC segment, ARM/custom silicon in data center, no disclosed Crescent Island design wins), and bearish near-term technicals. The bear only needs one quarter of disappointment to trigger multiple compression; the bull needs a clean sweep. That argues for trimming exposure, not exiting entirely — hence Underweight rather than Sell.
