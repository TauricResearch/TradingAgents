The bear's most damaging point wasn't that it's expensive in isolation — it's that the forward thesis requires near-perfect execution across multiple correlated conditions for years, and the market has *already* repriced the stock down 24% from $187 while the business accelerated. That disconnect tells you the market is pricing significant deceleration, and betting against that at this multiple is a high bar.

Second, the bear exposed two things the bull never fully refuted. The probability framework the bull leaned on for his +7.25% expected value was admittedly invented — flip the assumptions modestly and you get negative EV. And the bear correctly noted that the $128-130 stop is illusory protection against the single most likely adverse catalyst: a Q2 earnings gap-down. The stock's own history (167 to 122 in April) proves 9% stops get blown through. That undercuts the bull's central risk-management claim.

Third — and this was the bear's sharpest catch — our own technical research synthesis reportedly concludes HOLD with a cautious-to-slightly-bearish near-term bias, not BUY. The stock is clinging to a *declining* 50 SMA by $0.41, MACD histogram is contracting after a 100%-rejected Bollinger spike, and weekly + monthly TD-9 setups are building concurrently. The bull's MACD-crossover argument is legitimate but it's a single constructive signal against a stack of cautious ones.

Where I come down: the bull won the "is this a great business" debate, but the bear won the "is this a great entry today" debate. Those are different questions, and the bear was right to keep separating them. The SBC dilution (~$685M/yr, ~1.3% annual dilution that buybacks can't fully neutralize) and the A/R growing faster than revenue are yellow flags, not red ones, but they argue against paying a premium-of-the-premium right here.

This is genuinely balanced: extraordinary business, stretched valuation, fragile near-term technicals, and a real catalyst (Q2 earnings) only weeks away that could resolve it in either direction. That combination is the textbook definition of Hold — maintain exposure to a best-in-class franchise, but don't add aggressively into a declining moving average and a binary earnings event when our own technicals say wait.
