## 5. 💰 On the DOJ "Gift": The Bull's Math Has a Critical Assumption Error

My colleague made an interesting and creative argument about the Apple default payment relationship. He said: Google pays $15-26B annually for the Apple default. If they lose it, they lose $9-15B in annual revenue but save $15-26B in payments. Net positive.

I want to engage this with the precision it deserves.

The bull's math assumes that **losing the Apple default eliminates Google's payment obligation to Apple.** That is not how these agreements work. **The payment is for default placement — losing the placement doesn't mean Apple owes Google a refund.** The $15-26B in annual payments would stop *because Google stops being the default* — meaning Google loses both the revenue and the payment simultaneously ends, generating no savings. The savings only materialize if you assume Google would have continued paying for default placement that they no longer hold. **That's not a saving. That's the absence of a future expenditure that wouldn't occur anyway because the relationship has ended.**

Moreover, the bull's demographic argument cuts against his own DOJ dismissal. He argued that Apple device users are high-value and that the $77B quarterly ad revenue "growing 21.8%" disproves search monetization risk. But if Apple users represent 3-5% of total search revenue and Google loses them, **that's $9-15B annually** that doesn't just reduce revenue — it disproportionately reduces the highest-yield revenue. The advertising yield compression the bull dismissed as theoretical becomes real and immediate when Bing becomes the default on hundreds of millions of Apple devices.

## 6. 📉 The Technical Picture: Let Me Name the Specific Risk the Bull Is Minimizing

My colleague spent considerable time arguing that the ATM program won't cap every rally because management won't aggressively dilute their own holdings. I want to address this with a specific behavioral observation.

**Alphabet management's incentive structure under the ATM program is more complex than "they don't want to dilute themselves."** Executive compensation at Alphabet is heavily weighted toward RSUs — restricted stock units that vest over multi-year periods. When the ATM program deploys new shares into the market, management's *unvested* RSUs are diluted proportionally. However, management's *current compensation grants* are calibrated to account for this — meaning the board compensation committee adjusts grant sizes to offset the dilution effect.

In practice, this means **the personal wealth effect on management from ATM deployment is largely neutralized by grant size adjustments.** Management doesn't feel the dilution pain in the same way that outside shareholders do. The bull's behavioral argument — "management won't dilute themselves" — assumes a perfect alignment between management interests and shareholder interests on dilution. **That alignment is imperfect precisely because of the way executive compensation is structured.**

And on the technical setup itself: the bull acknowledged that a test of $301 is possible. At $301, GOOGL would have declined **50% from its peak of $408.61.** Even in the bull's framing — where the long-term trend remains intact — that represents a **26-month round trip to zero return** for investors who bought during the April-May earnings euphoria. **A 26-month round trip to zero is not a "brief dip." It's the investment thesis not working.** And with ATM supply continuously available, the recovery from $301 to $408 faces a different supply environment than the original run-up did.
