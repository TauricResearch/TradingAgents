## 💰 The SBC Double-Counting Accusation — My Colleague Got the Accounting Wrong

The bull accused me of "counting SBC twice" — once as an expense and once as dilution. This is incorrect, and the error matters.

**GAAP accounting treats SBC as an income statement expense.** This reduces GAAP net income. The forward P/E of 22x is calculated on **non-GAAP earnings** — which adds SBC back. So when an investor uses the 22x forward P/E to evaluate AVGO's valuation, they are using an earnings number that has had SBC stripped out.

**Having removed SBC from the earnings used in the multiple, you must separately account for the dilution it creates.** These are not the same calculation. The income statement adjustment captures the *compensation cost*. The dilution adjustment captures the *economic transfer* from existing shareholders to employees via share issuance. These are two distinct economic phenomena, and both must be captured in a complete analysis.

To be concrete: The forward EPS of $19.06 is non-GAAP. It excludes SBC of approximately $8.72B annually. If you add SBC back as a cost against that $19.06 non-GAAP EPS figure, you get approximately $14.50 in adjusted EPS. The effective forward multiple on adjusted earnings is approximately **27-28x** — not 22x.

**The bull's "reasonable 22x forward P/E" is only 22x if you accept non-GAAP earnings methodology in full. Once you reintroduce SBC costs, the multiple becomes 27-28x.** At that level, the valuation conversation looks substantially different, particularly when AI revenue growth just printed flat sequentially.

## 📊 The Final Technical Picture — What's Actually Happening Post-Earnings

Let me close the technical argument with one observation the bull hasn't addressed.

**A 15% earnings gap-down on 50M shares of volume creates a specific technical pattern: the "exhaustion gap followed by distribution."** This is distinct from the "breakaway gap" (beginning of a new trend) and the "continuation gap" (middle of an established trend). An exhaustion gap occurs at the peak of a strong move and signals that the buyers who drove the prior rally have been fully replaced by sellers.

The technical indicators my colleague cited — MACD at +17.46, RSI at 73, Bollinger Upper Band breach — were the *signals* of an exhaustion setup. The 15% gap-down was the *confirmation.* In technical analysis, once an exhaustion gap is confirmed by volume and price action of this magnitude, the subsequent trading pattern is typically:

1. Initial stabilization (SSR effect, short-term bounce)
2. Failed recovery attempt (overhead resistance from the gap zone, $460-$479)
3. Re-test of new lows or lower support levels ($350-$395 range)
4. Only then: base-building before potential recovery

The bull says post-earnings levels offer "better entry than pre-earnings." Technically, they're correct that RSI and Bollinger metrics have improved. But they're wrong about the *pattern* — what looks like a "reset to neutral" after a gap-down is often the beginning of a distribution range, not the start of a recovery. The neutral RSI after a gap-down isn't oversold opportunity; it's mid-range with unresolved overhead supply.

**I'm not predicting this plays out with certainty. I'm saying the technical risk pattern is materially worse after the earnings gap than the bull is acknowledging.**
