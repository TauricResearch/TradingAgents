Developers enroll with genuine intent, build applications, submit them to the App Store, and then discover that consumer adoption of new AI-native features takes quarters rather than weeks. The App Store deceleration we've been discussing is a consumer-side metric, not a developer-side metric. Enrollment data tells you whether the developer ecosystem found the WWDC announcement credible enough to commit. It does not tell you whether that commitment will reverse the consumer-side App Store growth trend that UBS has been flagging. That distinction matters for how you weight the enrollment signal in the upgrade decision, and the aggressive framing has been collapsing it throughout.

On the collar term structure, all three of us have agreed on extending two to three weeks beyond WWDC and I'm not going to relitigate that. It's the right structural decision. But the App Store execution risk scenario I just described plays out over quarters, not weeks. A two to three week collar extension does not protect you against the scenario where you upgrade on developer enthusiasm, the stock moves higher, and then two quarters later the Services deceleration becomes undeniable. The collar is event risk management. It is not thesis risk management. Those require different tools and different timelines, and the plan needs to be explicit about where the collar's protection ends and where ongoing position review takes over.
