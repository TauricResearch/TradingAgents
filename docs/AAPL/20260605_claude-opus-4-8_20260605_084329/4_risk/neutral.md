In the scenario where WWDC disappoints, the App Store trend immediately becomes the dominant de-rating narrative because the market now has both a concrete negative catalyst and a pre-existing deteriorating metric to anchor the thesis on. That asymmetry argues for precisely the structure we've been describing. Put protection that activates in the base case disappointment scenario before the App Store narrative takes hold as a secondary amplifier. And a call cap that is set generously enough to allow full participation in the scenario where the AI announcement reframes the Services story constructively. Those two specifications are not in conflict with each other. They are the same insight expressed from opposite directions of the distribution.

The narrative duration risk that the conservative analyst raised in the final round is the one genuinely unaddressed point that I think deserves direct engagement. The risk that the AI story is real but slower to monetize than current pricing assumes, that the valuation compression happens over two to four quarters rather than in the immediate post-WWDC window, that the collar expires before the gradual de-rating has run its course. That is a real risk and the conservative analyst is right to name it. But the response to narrative duration risk is not a more aggressive collar specification. A collar that expires two to three weeks post-event cannot protect against a two to four quarter valuation compression story regardless of where the strikes are set. The tool for narrative duration risk is the ongoing position review process that the plan already implies but does not explicitly specify. The collar handles the event risk. The quarterly earnings cadence, the App Store monthly data, the developer monetization signals that emerge over the subsequent two quarters, those are the inputs to the ongoing thesis review that determines whether the Overweight upgrade is warranted on a sustained basis or whether the WWDC enthusiasm was front-running a monetization story that is taking longer than expected to materialize. Those are different tools for different risk horizons, and conflating them by demanding a more expensive collar is not the answer.
