**Recommendation**: Underweight

**Rationale**: Alright, let me walk you through how I landed here, because this was a genuinely high-quality debate and I don't want to flatten the nuance.

The bull made a real case on business quality, and I give full credit where it's due: ARM is an exceptional franchise. 97.5% gross margins, an IP licensing model that taxes nearly every chip shipped, 22%+ revenue growth with sequential acceleration (Q4 FY2026 at $1.49B), $1.524B in operating cash flow up 283%, a fortress balance sheet with ~$3.1B net cash and zero financial debt, and genuine competitive validation from five independent hyperscalers (AWS Graviton, Microsoft Cobalt, Google Axion, Oracle/ByteDance, and now Nvidia Vera) all converging on ARM architecture. The Armv9 royalty-rate uplift is a real, structural, multi-year tailwind. The "structural lag" argument — that ARM's income statement trails its business development pipeline by 2-3 years — is the bull's single sharpest point, and it's legitimately true. None of this is mythology. This is a winning architecture.

But here's where the debate actually turned, and it turned on the bull's own numbers. When the bull ran an honest 10-year DCF in Round 8, they arrived at a ~$140-142B enterprise value against a $433B market cap — the stock trading at roughly 3x its own base-case discounted cash flow. To close that gap, the bull had to compress the discount rate to 9% on a beta-3.41 stock (CAPM would imply something far north of that) and push terminal growth to a level where the denominator essentially collapsed toward infinite value. The bear correctly flagged this as reverse-engineering a conclusion from the price. That's the tell. When you have to torture the inputs to justify the quote, the quote is ahead of the fundamentals.

The bear's probability-weighted intrinsic value of roughly $126/share is, frankly, too aggressive on the downside — it leans on conservative terminal assumptions and a high discount rate that I think understates ARM's durable moat. But the direction is right even if I'd soften the magnitude. And critically, the bull's own recommendation undercut the bull's own rating: they said to buy "most aggressively" at $315 (10 EMA) or $258 (Bollinger Middle Band), which are 23-37% below the current price. A BUY whose author tells you to wait for a 30%+ pullback to add is, functionally, a cautious stance dressed up in bull language.

The technical setup reinforces caution rather than driving the thesis: RSI at 82, price ~$29 above the upper Bollinger Band, trading at 2x the 50-day SMA after a 100% move in 20 trading days, with beta of 3.41. That doesn't make ARM a fundamental SELL — extended technicals can persist in genuine secular winners (the Nvidia 2023 parallel has merit) — but it does make $408 a poor risk/reward entry point.

On the specific battlegrounds: SBC dilution is real but the bull won the trajectory argument (sub-1% annual share dilution, SBC falling as a % of revenue) — the bear's "917x economic cash flow" double-counted dilution. R&D plateau is genuinely unresolved; the bear is right there's no management commentary supporting it, so I'd treat margin expansion as a hope, not a base case. China risk is bounded but live, and the bear's point that the U.S. Foreign Direct Product Rule reaches ARM chips through American manufacturing tools (as with Huawei) defeats the "British IP is immune" defense. SoftBank overhang is a real, if low-probability, supply risk.

So where does that leave me? I can't endorse SELL on a business growing 22%+ with this moat, this cash generation, and a strengthening structural thesis — selling a generational compounder outright on valuation alone is how you end up buying it back higher. But I equally can't endorse BUY when the bull's own DCF says the stock trades at 3x intrinsic value and the bull's own entry discipline points 30% lower. The strongest arguments — on both sides — converge on a single conclusion: great company, wrong price, near-term reward/risk skewed unfavorably. That's an Underweight: trim exposure, respect the franchise, and build the position back at sane levels.

For existing holders, trim 30-50% of the ARM position into current strength near $408 to lock in gains from the parabolic run and reduce exposure to a beta-3.41 name trading 2x its 50-day SMA.
