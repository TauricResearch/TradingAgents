## 4. The "Additive Royalty Layers" Defense Has a Hidden Assumption the Bull Glossed Over

My colleague's response to the R&D treadmill was elegant: each architecture generation adds a *new layer* of royalty revenue on top of existing layers, so the treadmill is actually a staircase. v8 royalties don't stop when v9 launches. The base is additive.

Here's the commercial reality. When v9 launches at 2x the v8 royalty rate, v8 becomes the "old" architecture. Licensees designing new products want the price of v8 to reflect its maturity — they push for rate reductions. ARM faces a choice: maintain v8 rates (and risk losing volume to RISC-V in cost-sensitive segments) or reduce v8 rates (and accept declining revenue per unit on the legacy base).

**The "additive staircase" only holds if ARM can maintain pricing on legacy architectures while simultaneously raising rates on new ones.** In competitive markets, this pricing power erodes. We can already see it in the mix: ARM's volume growth in China and IoT segments — the v8 heartland — is being challenged by RISC-V precisely because ARM's legacy rates are still too high relative to the value delivered by mature architecture. The staircase has a leaky bottom step.

And here's the compounding problem: **as v9 becomes the new standard, it too will face this pressure from v10.** The "additive layers" thesis requires each layer to hold its price permanently. The competitive dynamics of mature architectures say this never happens. The bull's staircase is actually an escalator — it goes up and down simultaneously, with the bottom steps declining as the top steps rise.
