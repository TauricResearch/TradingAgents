**Recommendation**: Overweight

**Rationale**: Alright, let me cut through nine rounds of back-and-forth and tell you where I actually land on NU.

First, what's not in dispute. Both analysts agreed — and the bear explicitly conceded — that this is a genuinely exceptional business. 57% revenue growth, 56.5% net income growth, 48.2% operating margin, 30% ROE, 135 million customers, $23B cash against $1.9B debt, and a forward PE around 10x with a PEG of 0.76. When the bear's own closing says "in 24-36 months, NU may well be substantially higher than today," the fundamental debate is effectively over. The disagreement collapsed into timing and price, not the durability of the franchise.

Now, the bull won the core arguments on substance. The provision math scare turned out to be largely explained by IFRS 9 lifetime-loss accounting on a fast-growing, seasoning origination book — and crucially, net income is still compounding at 56% AFTER provisioning, which is hard to square with a credit book that's quietly imploding. The FX argument is real but the bull correctly noted that 57% USD-reported growth already absorbs whatever currency drag occurred. The "six departures = dysfunction" thesis was never backed by evidence on why anyone left, and the financials show acceleration, not deterioration — not the fingerprint of an organization in crisis. The U.S. expansion cost the bear quantified at ~$300M is roughly 8.6% of operating cash flow, fully self-funded, and the margins hold regardless. These were the bear's strongest fundamental shots and they mostly missed.

But — and this is why I'm not going to full Buy — the bear made two points that I refuse to wave away. One: NU is making fresh 52-week lows in a Stage 4 downtrend while the S&P sits at all-time highs. That idiosyncratic underperformance in a roaring bull market is a real signal that the market is pricing something the bulls are discounting, and you don't get extra points for being early into a confirmed downtrend. Two: there is a genuine, calendar-bound event cluster right in front of us — the July 13 CFO handover and the Q2 2026 print that will be the first quarter to incorporate the freshest macro assumptions into the ECL model. The bear's expected-value framing around waiting was the most disciplined analytical work in the entire debate, even if I think they overstated the probability of the adverse leg.

So here's my synthesis: the bull won the long-term value argument decisively, but the bear won the near-term risk-management argument. That combination — exceptional asymmetric value plus real, dated event risk and an ugly tape — is exactly what an Overweight is built for. I'm not going to Hold, because the evidence is genuinely lopsided in favor of the business and the valuation; sitting flat would ignore a PEG of 0.76 on a self-funding 56% grower. But I'm not going Buy/max-conviction either, because committing full size into a 52-week-low downtrend ahead of two binary catalysts, with a credible $10 institutional target below us, is poor risk discipline. Overweight lets us commit to the stronger side of the debate while respecting that the bear correctly identified where the near-term landmines are.
