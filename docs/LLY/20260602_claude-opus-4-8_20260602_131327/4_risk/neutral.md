On the options question, both analysts ended up agreeing that a genuine cost-benefit analysis should be run rather than a rhetorical dismissal. I agree with that conclusion. But I want to add something neither of them said clearly enough. The right options structure here is not the collar the neutral analyst proposed, because the aggressive analyst is correct that selling the breakout call at $1,149 caps your upside in the exact scenario where conviction-based capital should be making its most money. It is also not pure long puts, because the aggressive analyst is correct that elevated implied volatility in a pre-binary environment makes the premium expensive. The right structure, if options are used at all, is a put spread rather than outright puts. Buying a put at $1,000 and selling a put at $850 would cost significantly less than buying the $1,000 put outright, because you're selling back the protection below $850 where the truly catastrophic scenario would have to materialize. That put spread directly addresses the technical report's $760 to $836 downside scenario while dramatically reducing the premium cost relative to outright puts. If you can implement that spread for less than 1.5 percent of the core position value, you should do it. If the premium exceeds 2 percent, you accept the gap risk in the core position and manage it through the two-thirds tranche sizing rather than outright hedging.

If all three are trending positively, execute the two-thirds tranches as planned. If one shows deterioration, reduce the fill to one-third of the intended increment.
