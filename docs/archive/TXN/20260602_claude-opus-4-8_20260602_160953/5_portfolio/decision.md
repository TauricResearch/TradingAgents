**Investment Thesis**: The strongest arguments on both sides largely cancel out, which is precisely what a Hold is for. The bull case is grounded in measurable, not narrative, data: FCF inflected from -$274M (Q1 2025) to +$844M (Q1 2026), a ~$1.1B four-quarter improvement; CapEx declined across four consecutive quarters from a $1.305B peak to $676M; revenue grew 18.6% YoY with gross margins recovering to 58%; BofA carries a $370 target (~26% upside from $293, correctly measured from current price not the May peak); and TXN holds a named design win on Nvidia's 800V power architecture. The MACD line at +14.6 and a steeply rising 50-day SMA $40 below price confirm the medium-term uptrend is intact, with the April earnings gap still unfilled.

But the bear landed real, unrefuted damage. The dividend has been debt-funded for three straight years (~$9B cumulative gap, total debt ~$14B), leaving a ~$1.6B annual shortfall against the $5B dividend even on the bull's optimistic ~$3.4B annualized FCF. At 32.7x forward earnings, the $9.41 forward EPS is literally TXN's 2022 peak-cycle number, leaving zero margin of safety. The Conservative Analyst's sharpest point survived rebuttal: the gross margin gap from 58% to the ~63% required for peak EPS is 500bps, and at the observed 120bps-per-four-quarters pace it is roughly 17 quarters away on a linear basis. The bull's S-curve fixed-cost-absorption counter is mechanically plausible but unproven — we have no public fab utilization data confirming the inflection has arrived, which the Neutral Analyst correctly flagged as the central unknown. The market's own behavior is instructive: it took 9.6% off the stock in five sessions on nothing but an RSI reset from 77, demonstrating it is a hair-trigger multiple-compression machine at this valuation — meaning a Q2 miss could combine an EPS estimate cut with multiple compression to drive ~19% downside to ~$238 before the $276 stop reliably protects, and gap risk from China/geopolitical shocks is asymmetrically skewed to the downside.

The probability-weighted expected return is genuinely positive but only modestly so — the debate converged on roughly 5-9% depending on whether one accepts the bull's 60% central-case probability (which is anchored on narrative quality rather than the base rate of high-multiple semis stumbling on at least one quarterly variable) or the more cautious calibration. That is only marginally above the risk-free rate for a 1.3-beta name, while the bear's downside scenarios require multiple things to go wrong rather than being base cases. That two-sided asymmetry is insufficient to justify either chasing the dip aggressively or dumping a quality compounder mid-inflection. Hold at disciplined size, let the next print confirm the CapEx normalization, gross-margin S-curve, and dividend-coverage trajectory, and pre-commit to the decision rules: CapEx ≤$700M with revenue >15% and gross margins >59% validates upgrading toward Overweight; CapEx >$900M, decelerating revenue, or flat-to-declining margins justifies moving to Underweight.
