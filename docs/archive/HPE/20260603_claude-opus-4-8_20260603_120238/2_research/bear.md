## 🎯 The "Post-Earnings Drift" Citation Is the Bull's Most Dangerous Sleight of Hand

My colleague invoked academic research on post-earnings announcement drift to justify raising my Scenario A probability from 35% to 52-55%. He cited Ball & Brown and Sloan. Let me tell you what that research actually says — because the bull either hasn't read it carefully or is hoping you haven't.

**Post-earnings announcement drift (PEAD) describes a phenomenon where stocks with large earnings surprises continue drifting in the direction of the surprise over the subsequent 60-90 days.** The effect is real, documented, and accepted. But here's what the bull conveniently omitted about the PEAD literature:

**First, PEAD is measured over 60-90 trading days — not 12-18 months.** The bull is using a 60-90 day phenomenon to justify an 18-month investment thesis. That's applying a short-term momentum effect to a long-term fundamental argument. The academic literature does not support using PEAD as a basis for 18-month return probability estimates. It supports a modest near-term drift — typically 3-5% over 60 days in the top earnings surprise decile. Not a 50%+ probability of achieving a 25%+ return over 18 months.

**Second, PEAD is substantially weaker — and sometimes reverses — for stocks that have already experienced massive pre-earnings momentum.** HPE was up 80%+ *before* the Q2 earnings report drove the final leg. The academic evidence on PEAD specifically notes that stocks where most of the earnings surprise has already been anticipated in price movement show weaker post-announcement drift. HPE's price had been pricing in improved AI fundamentals for months. The "surprise" component that drives PEAD was partially pre-announced by the price action itself.

**Third, and most decisively:** PEAD research is conducted on average returns across large samples of stocks. It tells you the *average* company with a top-decile earnings surprise drifts higher. It tells you nothing about the specific distribution of outcomes for a company with RSI 91, 88% above the 50-day SMA, $16.77 billion in net debt, and declining gross margins. **Average-sample research cannot be applied to extreme-outlier individual cases.** The bull is using population statistics to make a case about an outlier.

When you correct for these three factors, the defensible Scenario A probability for HPE specifically — not the average top-decile earnings surprise stock — is approximately **30-40%**, not the 52-55% the bull asserts. My original 35% was, if anything, slightly generous.
