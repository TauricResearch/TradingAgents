**Rationale**: Alright, let me cut through this debate, because both sides were genuinely strong and I want to be honest about where each one landed punches.

The HBM co-design switching costs, the 5+ year supply ramp at SK Hynix, and the public multi-year hyperscaler capex commitments give the demand story real legs beyond a single quarter.

But the bear did NOT lose this debate, and anyone reading it as a clean bull victory is fooling themselves. When the bull's own non-catastrophic base case implies a meaningful drawdown, you cannot rate this a high-conviction Buy. The bear also correctly flagged that this is a cyclical commodity-adjacent business at peak margins, that $105 EPS likely reflects peak-cycle rather than mid-cycle earnings, that MU is the third-place HBM supplier (so margin support is partly a scarcity premium, not a pure moat), and that the technical setup — RSI bearish divergence, price riding the upper Bollinger Band 33% above the 20-day SMA, a $57 daily ATR, Beta 1.92 — adds genuine near-term downside risk.

Where I come down: the bull won the *direction* but the bear won the *risk management*. The structural demand story, the discounted forward multiple, and the transformed balance sheet tilt the probabilities favorably for a 12-24 month horizon — even the bear's own expected-value model produced a positive (+11%) expected return. That argues against Sell or Underweight. But the elevated valuation after a +228% run, the peak-cycle margin question, the third-place HBM positioning, the inventory-build risk as hyperscaler orders get digested, and the stretched technicals argue strongly against a full Buy. The honest synthesis is constructive-but-disciplined: accumulate gradually on weakness rather than chase strength, keep position sizing modest, and respect the volatility. That's an Overweight, not a Buy — committing to the stronger side of the debate while refusing to ignore the very real asymmetry the bear exposed using the bull's own numbers.
