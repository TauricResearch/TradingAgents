**Rationale**: Alright, let me walk you through how I'm landing this one, because both analysts brought their A-game and I don't want to wave away the strong points on either side.

The bull's core case is genuinely powerful and, importantly, it rests on hard reported numbers rather than projection: Q1 2026 revenue +33.1% YoY, EPS +62%, operating margins north of 40%, $115.8B in operating cash flow, $81.2B in cash, and a forward P/E of ~16.5x with a PEG below 1.0. The bull also made the single most important conceptual point of the debate: FCF compression here is a deliberate reinvestment choice, not business deterioration. Operating cash flow grew from $91.3B to $115.8B in a year — the cash-generating machine is accelerating, and capex is temporarily outrunning it. That's value creation in progress, not a melting ice cube.

The bear, to their credit, landed several legitimate blows that I refuse to dismiss. First, the FCF decline (15% in FY2025 on 22% revenue growth) is real, and the capex-per-incremental-revenue math ($1.37 spent per $1.00 of new revenue in Q1) is a fair flag that the returns on the newest infrastructure are unproven at the scale being deployed. Second — and this is the bear's strongest argument — the technical structure IS genuinely broken: price below the 10-EMA, 50-SMA, and 200-SMA, with the heaviest volume days consistently on the downside. The 200-day SMA as an institutional-mandate trigger is a sound mechanical observation. Third, the macro backdrop (NFP/rate risk, Iran-driven inflation pressure on ad budgets, beta of 1.24) creates real near-term binary risk. Fourth, the EU AI Act is qualitatively different from past procedural fines and deserves a genuine risk premium.

But here's where the bear's case ultimately undercut itself. They conceded — explicitly — that META "probably will" reach $750, just "eventually." Once you grant that the destination is materially higher and the business is "genuinely extraordinary," your SELL recommendation collapses into a market-timing argument: sell at $600, dodge a possible dip to $575-583, and re-buy at $665 after the 200-day reclaim. That's asking the trader to nail two timing decisions correctly to save ~$25-65 of downside on a stock the bear admits is heading to $750. The asymmetry doesn't justify exiting a high-quality compounder at a below-market multiple. The bear's own honest probability-weighting landed near breakeven — which is an argument against adding aggressively, not an argument for selling a franchise asset.

So where does that leave me? I can't endorse the bull's "BUY with full conviction at $600 today" because the technical breakdown and live macro catalysts are real, and the bear is right that there's no near-term identified catalyst to flip the trend before Q2 earnings. Buying the full position into a confirmed bearish stack with NFP days away is imprudent. But I equally can't endorse SELL on a business with 33% revenue growth, 40% margins, a 0.91 PEG, and a fortress balance sheet that the bear themselves believes is going higher. The valuation IS the margin of safety here.

The honest synthesis: this is a constructive, accumulate-on-weakness situation, not an all-in or get-out situation. The fundamental trajectory clearly favors the bull; the near-term tactical setup clearly favors patience. That combination is what Overweight is built for — lean into the name, but phase the entry to respect the technicals and macro the bear correctly flagged.
