**Rationale**: Alright, let me walk you through how I'm reading this debate, because both sides argued well and I don't want to pretend it was a blowout.

The bull's core case is genuinely strong on fundamentals: 33% YoY revenue growth in Q1 2026, $10.44 quarterly EPS, ~41% operating margins, $115B in annual operating cash flow, $81B cash, a forward P/E around 17x, and a PEG under 1.0. On top of that, the AI monetization story isn't purely speculative — the value optimization suite is already at a ~$20B run-rate and doubled YoY, which is the single most important data point in the whole debate because it directly answers the bear's "AI CapEx returns are unproven" objection. The advertising platform moat across 3B+ daily users is real and benefits from budget concentration when advertisers rationalize spend toward highest-ROI channels. Sell-side targets of $775-810 and the bull's compromise DCF (intrinsic value in the $640-720 range even at a 9-9.5% discount rate) both sit above the current $597.

The bear, to their credit, landed several legitimate punches that keep me from going all the way to Buy. Three concerns survived the back-and-forth: (1) The macro timing argument is genuinely valid — Q1 budgets were largely committed before the tariff/Iran/consumer-confidence deterioration that intensified in April, so Q2 is a cleaner and as-yet-unconfirmed test. (2) The shareholder-return picture has objectively weakened — buybacks went from $12.75B in Q1 2025 to zero in Q1 2026, and FCF/CapEx compression is real (CapEx consuming ~60% of operating cash flow). (3) The technical setup is unfavorable near-term — the stock is below every major moving average, failed to reclaim $618 even on the Business Agent catalyst, and sits just above $590 Bollinger support with $547 as a realistic downside if Q2 disappoints.

Where the bear over-reached: the "wait for $547 after a Q2 miss" thesis is essentially a bet on bad news arriving, and they never defined a falsification condition for their own bearishness while demanding one from the bull. Their EMH-based "the market has decided $597 is fair" argument is weakest precisely during the post-macro-shock window they themselves describe. And their 10% discount rate was on the high side — even a neutral ~9.25% lands intrinsic value above the current price, meaning the bear's own framework doesn't actually support a Sell.

Net assessment: the fundamental, valuation, and competitive evidence clearly favors the bull, and the AI monetization is already showing up in real revenue — not just a promise. But the bear's macro-timing and Q2-binary-event concerns are real enough, and the technical/capital-return picture cautious enough, that I can't justify maximum conviction or aggressive sizing right here. That balance lands me at Overweight: constructive, building exposure, but deliberately leaving room to add on either Q2 confirmation or a pullback into the $547-570 zone. The arguments that carried it were the $20B proven AI revenue line and the below-fair-value multiple on accelerating earnings; the arguments that capped it were the unconfirmed Q2 macro test and the deteriorating near-term technical/capital-return signals.
