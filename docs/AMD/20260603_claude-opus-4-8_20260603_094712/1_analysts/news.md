Buffett's interest in and subsequent sale of TSMC illustrates the geopolitical risk embedded in the AI chip supply chain. ## 4. 📈 Sector Dynamics & Investment Positioning

### 4.1 AI Infrastructure Build-Out — Sustained Tailwind
The AI data center build-out remains the single most powerful secular tailwind for AMD. Hyperscaler capex commitments from Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta are multi-year programs that directly generate demand for AMD's EPYC CPUs and Instinct GPU accelerators. The Helios platform launch at Computex is AMD's most direct challenge yet to Nvidia's dominance.

### 4.2 AMD's Differentiation Strategy
AMD is effectively pursuing a **two-vector AI strategy**:
1. **GPU acceleration** (MI-series) for model training and inference
2. **CPU leadership** (EPYC) for agentic AI workloads, which require high core-count, memory-intensive compute for running orchestration layers, agents, and inference at scale

The CFO's emphasis on agentic AI driving CPU demand is particularly notable — it suggests AMD has a **competitive moat in a segment Nvidia cannot dominate** with GPUs alone.

### 4.3 Valuation Risk
Despite strong fundamental momentum, AMD (like the broader semiconductor sector) has likely priced in significant near-term execution success. Any guidance miss, product delay, or macro shock could trigger a sharp correction given stretched valuations.
