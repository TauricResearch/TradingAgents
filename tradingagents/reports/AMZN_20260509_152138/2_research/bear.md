
Bear Analyst: # 🐻 Bear Case for Amazon (AMZN) — Why the Rocket Ship Has a Structural Crack in the Hull

---

Look, I appreciate the passion in the bull case. Truly. It's a beautifully constructed narrative. But that's exactly what it is — a *narrative*. And right now, this stock is trading on story, not substance. Let me show you why the numbers, the technicals, and the competitive reality all tell a more sobering story than the bull wants you to see.

---

## 🚨 Let's Start With the Elephant in the Room: That Q1 "Earnings Beat" Is Largely a Mirage

My bull colleague just spent considerable time assuring us that even stripping out the $15.7 billion investment gain, normalized earnings grew 22.8% YoY. Fine. Let me accept that math completely and still make the bear case.

**Here's what actually happened:** Amazon reported $30.3 billion in net income, and the market cheered. Goldman Sachs — not some fringe newsletter, but one of the most respected institutional voices on Wall Street — came out and said *wait, slow down, this number is misleading.* And they were right.

Think about what that means structurally. **Amazon's reported EPS of $2.78 includes roughly $1.45 in gains from securities that may never repeat.** Investors are pricing forward earnings based on a headline number that's been inflated by a one-time event. The Forward P/E of 27.6x sounds reasonable — until you realize the earnings baseline investors are extrapolating from has been artificially elevated. The *true* forward multiple on normalized earnings is meaningfully higher than what bulls are advertising.

And the bull's response? "That's Amazon's capital allocation genius." With respect, calling a realized investment gain "genius" to obscure the fact that your operating earnings disappointed expectations isn't analysis — it's spin.

---

## 💸 The Free Cash Flow Reality Is Far Worse Than Bulls Admit

Let me hit this hard because the bull glossed right over it: **Amazon burned -$18.2 billion in free cash flow in Q1 2026 alone.** Not for the year. For *one quarter.*

The bull says this is "intentional front-loading." Fine. Let's stress-test that argument.

- Q1 2026 CapEx: **$44.2 billion** — up 76.8% from $25.0 billion just one year ago
- Q1 2026 FCF: **-$18.2 billion**
- Long-term debt: **$209.9 billion** — up 57.5% YoY
- New debt issued in Q1 alone: **$53.4 billion**

The bull says "Amazon did this with AWS and look how that turned out." That's a seductive analogy — but it's historically incomplete. When Amazon was building AWS, interest rates were near zero, the debt burden was a fraction of today's scale, and the competitive landscape wasn't forcing simultaneous massive CapEx from Microsoft, Google, and a dozen well-funded AI startups. 

**The playbook from 2006 does not automatically transfer to 2026.** The cost of capital is higher, the competitive response is faster, and the margin for error is narrower. Issuing $53.4 billion in new debt in a single quarter to fund investments whose ROI timeline is measured in *years* is not genius — it's a bet. A large, potentially very costly bet.

And let me put this in perspective: **Amazon's debt-to-equity ratio is 53.3.** Not 53.3% — 53.3x. In an environment where interest rates remain elevated, that leverage profile deserves serious scrutiny, not a wave of the hand.

---

## ☁️ The AWS Outage Is NOT "Just Noise" — It's a Competitive Opening

My colleague dismisses the AWS outage as a "speed bump." I want to push back on this directly, because the bull is conflating two very different things: *historical* customer stickiness and *future* customer behavior in a world of increasingly viable alternatives.

Yes, switching costs are real. Yes, enterprises don't migrate off AWS overnight. **But here's what the bull is missing: enterprise procurement cycles are 12-18 months long.** The Coinbase outage, the trading platform disruptions — those aren't just embarrassing headlines. They are being documented in boardrooms and IT procurement meetings right now. The next RFP cycle for cloud infrastructure? AWS just handed Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud a *gift-wrapped talking point.*

And here's the structural concern that never gets discussed in bull cases: **AWS's market share has been gradually declining.** AWS was at ~33% in 2021. It's now at ~31%. Azure has been consistently gaining ground. The trend is slow, but it is real, and it is persistent. Every outage, every reliability incident, nudges that trajectory further.

The bull says "CapEx is going toward fixing reliability." Maybe. But that same CapEx is also driving the -$18.2B FCF we just discussed. You can't simultaneously use CapEx as evidence of *future strength* and dismiss it as a *current cost concern*. Pick one.

## 📊 The Technical Picture Is Screaming "Distribution," Not "Accumulation"

I want to specifically address the bull's technical argument because this is where I think the most dangerous complacency is lurking for investors buying at current levels.

**RSI at 75.13. Let me say that again: 75.13.**

The bull says "in strong secular trends, RSI can stay elevated." That is technically true. But let's look at what the data actually shows:

- RSI hit **81.14 on May 6th** — that's extreme overbought territory
- It's already pulled back to 75.13 by May 8th — **that's a lower high forming while price is still elevated**
- MACD peaked at **13.19 on May 6th** and has declined to **12.53 by May 8th** — momentum is rolling over

This is textbook **momentum divergence**. Price is holding near highs, but the underlying momentum indicators are already beginning to deteriorate. The bull calls this "a sign of momentum." Technical analysts call it a *warning sign of distribution.*

And consider the price context: **AMZN is trading 19.3% above its 200-day SMA.** Historically, mean reversion from that level of extension is not a question of *if* — it's a question of *when* and *how much.* The 200 SMA sits at $228.52. That's a potential drawdown of roughly **16% from current levels** if we simply revert to that long-term average — and that's without any fundamental deterioration.

The bull's "strong support at VWMA $264.72" is only $8 below current price. That's a 2.9% cushion. Not exactly inspiring for a stock with a beta of 1.468. **One bad macro data point, one more AWS incident, one analyst downgrade — and that "support" evaporates before you can execute a stop-loss.**

## 🏭 Supply Chain Services: Exciting Story, Dangerous Assumptions

I'll give the bull credit — the Amazon Supply Chain Services launch is genuinely interesting. But let me inject some reality into the "$1.3 trillion TAM" excitement.

**First, the margin reality.** Logistics is a notoriously low-margin business. UPS operates at roughly 9-10% operating margins. FedEx at around 8%. Amazon's own fulfillment and transportation segment has historically been a *cost center* that drags on overall margins, not a profit engine. The bull is assuming that by opening this to third parties, it transforms into a high-margin SaaS-like business overnight. That's not how logistics works.

**Second, the competitive response.** UPS and FedEx are not standing still. They have decades of customer relationships, regulatory expertise, and international networks. The idea that Amazon simply "enters logistics" like it entered cloud computing ignores the structural differences between the two industries. Cloud infrastructure benefits from enormous economies of scale and near-zero marginal costs for additional customers. Physical logistics doesn't scale the same way — every additional shipment has real variable costs.

**Third, the margin compression risk.** As Simply Wall St. specifically flagged in the social media report: this launch raises "new questions for margins and growth." Entering a new, capital-intensive, low-margin business at precisely the moment when you're already burning -$18.2B in quarterly FCF is not a "hidden catalyst" — it's an additional strain on an already stretched balance sheet.

## 🤖 AI Investments: The Bull's Crown Jewel May Be a Trap

The bull's most passionate argument is around AI — Trainium chips, Bedrock, the Anthropic investment. And look, directionally, being in AI is right. But let me ask the question that should be keeping Amazon investors up at night:

**What if the AI infrastructure buildout doesn't generate the expected returns?**

This isn't hypothetical fear-mongering. The data in our own reports shows that hyperscalers issued **$121 billion in bonds in 2025 alone — 4x the five-year average** — to fund AI CapEx. That means the *entire sector* is simultaneously making the same bet. When every competitor is spending at maximum capacity on the same technology, the competitive advantages from that spending get competed away faster.

Microsoft has Copilot embedded across the entire Office 365 ecosystem — that's 400 million commercial users. Google has Gemini with a search moat that Amazon simply doesn't have. **Amazon's AI story is compelling, but it's competing against two entrenched incumbents with structural distribution advantages that no amount of CapEx can simply buy.**

And Anthropic? The $4 billion Anthropic investment is an interesting hedge. But Anthropic also takes investment from Google. Claude is available on multiple platforms. This is not an exclusive moat — it's a minority stake in an AI lab that has multiple corporate patrons, none of whom have exclusive claims on its technology.

## 💰 Valuation: "Cheaper Than It Looks" Is Doing a Lot of Heavy Lifting

The bull's valuation argument centers on Forward P/E of 27.6x being reasonable for 16.6% revenue growth. Let me complicate that picture significantly.

**Price-to-Book of 6.64x.** In a capital-intensive business that is rapidly accumulating debt and spending aggressively on physical infrastructure, book value matters. At 6.64x book, you are paying a massive premium for assets that are depreciating, a brand that is already dominant (so limited additional pricing power from brand expansion), and future cash flows that are — as we've established — deeply uncertain.

**PEG of 1.91.** The bull says growth justifies this. But the PEG ratio assumes the "G" in the denominator is sustainable. Amazon's revenue growth will almost certainly decelerate as the base grows larger. **At $742.8 billion in TTM revenue, growing 16.6% requires generating an incremental $123 billion in new revenue each year.** The law of large numbers is not Amazon's friend. Every percentage point of growth gets geometrically harder to achieve.

And critically — **the forward EPS of $9.87 used to calculate that 27.6x Forward P/E is based on analyst consensus estimates that include those elevated, non-recurring investment gains in their baseline.** If analysts are extrapolating from an inflated Q1 2026 figure, the *real* forward multiple could be 30x or higher on normalized earnings.

## 🌍 The Macro Tailwinds Aren't As Clean As the Bull Suggests

The bull points to falling oil prices, strong employment, and consumer spending. Let me offer the other side of each of those points.

**Falling oil prices:** Yes, lower fuel costs help logistics margins. But oil prices are falling partly because of demand concerns and geopolitical uncertainty — factors that could simultaneously hurt consumer spending and enterprise IT budgets. You can't selectively extract the one positive from a macro development and ignore the context.

**Strong employment:** The report itself acknowledges a **K-shaped economic recovery** — high-income consumers are spending, low-income consumers are constrained. Amazon's bread-and-butter is mass-market e-commerce, which skews toward price-sensitive consumers. Prime membership renewals, discretionary product purchases, and advertising spend from small businesses — all of these are sensitive to low-income consumer health, which is under pressure.

**AI investment boom:** Framed as a tailwind for AWS. But remember — the same report notes that hyperscalers are borrowing at 4x historical rates to fund this boom. **Debt-funded investment cycles don't go on forever.** When the credit cycle turns, or when AI ROI doesn't materialize on schedule, the companies most exposed to leveraged AI CapEx face the sharpest corrections. Amazon, with $209.9B in long-term debt and -$18.2B quarterly FCF, is near the top of that exposure list.

The bull wants you to think like a long-term investor while simultaneously pointing to a 37% two-month run as evidence of momentum. You can't have both.

**My recommendation: SELL or at minimum AVOID at current levels.** Let the mean reversion play out, let the AI investment narrative prove itself with actual cash flow, and let the technical indicators reset. The $228-$231 SMA zone offers a far better risk-adjusted entry for those who truly believe the long-term bull case.

Because here's the thing about rockets: the ones that run out of fuel mid-flight don't drift gently back to Earth. 🐻
