My recommendation: Sell DASH (trim/exit equity exposure now)

Rationale (why I’m committing to Sell)
What decides it for me is the combination of (a) strong downtrend structure and (b) an execution-heavy, expectation-heavy fundamental setup. In similar situations, the costly mistake is “paying” for hope while the tape is still screaming risk—especially around integration windows where headlines and guidance shifts can gap the stock. The bull’s plan is actually an argument against buying common stock right now: if hedges are “mandatory,” that’s the market telling you the equity line is not the clean instrument at this moment. If you want upside, define the risk with options; don’t ride the full downside with unhedged shares in a confirmed downtrend.

You didn’t provide any past reflections to learn from, so I’ll just be explicit about the behavioral trap I want you to avoid here: averaging down in a structurally weak trend while waiting for “eventual” fundamental validation. That tends to compound losses and shrink flexibility.

Strategic actions (concrete implementation plan)

Step 1: Decide what you are today
If you’re currently long DASH:
1) Reduce now. If it’s a trading position, exit fully. If it’s an investment position, cut to a token “watch position” only (something small enough that you won’t rationalize holding through another leg down).
2) If you insist on keeping shares, hedge immediately (not later). A 30–90 day protective put or a collar (sell an out-of-the-money call to finance a put) is the right structure while the chart is below the 10-EMA/VWMA and integration risk is unresolved.
3) Don’t add shares on the way down. If you want to “buy the dip,” do it via defined-risk call spreads instead (see Step 3).

If you’re not currently long:
1) Do not buy common stock yet.
2) Either stand aside and wait for objective trend confirmation (see “Re-entry triggers”), or trade tactically (short setup), or use options to express upside with capped downside.

Step 2: If you want a bearish trade (tactical short)
Only do this if you actually trade actively and can follow stops.
1) Look to enter on a failed rally into resistance, not at random levels. The bear’s levels are logical: the 10-EMA (~162.6) and VWMA (~167.2) zone.
2) Use volatility-aware risk. ATR was cited near ~7.9, which is big—so size small. A reasonable stop framework is ~1.5 ATR above entry (about 12 points). Example: short near 163, stop around 175.
3) Profit-taking plan: first cover zone around ~140; second zone around ~125; trail once in profit so you don’t give back gains in a high-ATR name.
4) Hard rule: cap loss per trade at 1–2% of account.

Step 3: If you want bullish exposure without owning the downtrend
This is the compromise that respects both sides: bullish optionality, bearish tape.
1) Use a long-dated call spread (roughly 9–12 months). This expresses the bull thesis (re-rating if execution improves) while keeping max loss limited to premium paid.
2) Alternatively, sell a cash-secured put only at a level you truly want to own (the bear suggested thinking closer to ~140). But be honest: if you’re not thrilled to be assigned, don’t sell the put.

Step 4: Re-entry triggers (when I’d flip from Sell to Buy)
I would only consider buying shares (not just options) if both technicals and evidence improve:
1) Technical: DASH closes above the 10-EMA and VWMA and holds for multiple sessions, with momentum improving (MACD/RSI regime improving; RSI sustaining >50 is a clean filter).
2) Fundamental: clear signs the acquisition is integrating without margin damage—either improved guidance, credible KPIs, or at minimum margin/FCF stability across a couple of reports.
If you get those, you’re not “catching the bottom,” but you are buying a higher-probability trend change—which is usually the better trade when expectations and volatility are high.

Step 5: Risk controls you’ll follow no matter what
1) Position size smaller than you think you need. This stock’s swings (high ATR) make oversizing the #1 practical error.
2) No unhedged holding through binary events if you’re already unsure (earnings/integration updates). If you can’t define the downside, you don’t actually control the risk.
3) If you re-enter, scale only after the market confirms (not before).

Actionable bottom line
Sell/trim DASH equity now. If you want to stay involved, replace stock exposure with defined-risk upside (call spread) until DASH proves it can reclaim trend levels and until integration/margin concerns show real progress. This is one of those setups where “being early” is indistinguishable from “being wrong” unless you cap the downside.
