I’m siding with the bear analyst here, and my recommendation is Sell SPY tactically.

The bull made a solid case that SPY is still in a healthy primary uptrend. Price is above the 10 EMA, 50 SMA, and 200 SMA, both longer moving averages are rising, MACD is positive, and the market has absorbed macro noise better than a weak market would. I agree with that part. This does not look like a broken market, and it does not look like a major top has already been confirmed.

But the bear made the more decision-useful argument: trend quality is not the same thing as entry quality. At 708.72, SPY is stretched above its short- and medium-term support, RSI is 72.21, and the near-term upside looks limited relative to the downside if it simply mean-reverts. That’s the key point. The debate wasn’t really about whether SPY is a good long-term asset. It was about whether this is a good place to put money to work right now. On that question, the bear was stronger.

What really tips it for me is the asymmetry. If SPY only has modest room toward the upper Bollinger area around 718.09, but a normal pullback could take it back toward 691.46 or even 674.54, then the reward-to-risk for fresh buying is weak. Add in the fact that SPY is still sensitive to oil and geopolitical headlines, and this becomes a market I’d rather sell into strength than chase.

So my call is straightforward: Sell SPY if this is a tactical trading position, or at minimum trim SPY if you’re sitting on gains. I would not be initiating a new long in SPY at this level.

The rationale is simple. The bull is probably right about the bigger trend, but the bear is right about the current setup. I don’t want to repeat the common mistake of treating a strong asset like an automatic buy after a sharp V-shaped run. Since no prior mistake reflections were provided, the lesson I’m explicitly applying here is this: don’t confuse “good market” with “good entry.” That discipline matters more than trying to squeeze the last 1% out of an extended move.

Here’s how I’d implement it.

If you already own SPY as a trading position, I’d sell 30% to 50% of the position now into strength. On the remaining SPY, I’d use a stop slightly below the 10 EMA zone, around 691, to protect against a sharper momentum unwind. If SPY closes decisively below that area, I’d reduce further. If SPY then loses the 50 SMA area near 674.54, I’d exit the rest of the tactical position.

If you do not own SPY, I would stay patient and not buy SPY here. I’d wait for one of two things. Either SPY pulls back into the 691 to 675 area and shows support, at which point you can scale in gradually, or SPY proves the bears wrong by breaking out cleanly above the 718 to 720 area with follow-through, in which case momentum is re-confirmed and you can buy a smaller starter position.

If you want to express a bearish view on SPY, I’d keep it tactical and risk-defined. The primary trend is still up, so I would not put on a big naked short. A small put spread or a small short with a tight stop above recent highs makes more sense than an aggressive bearish bet.

For position sizing, keep it smaller than usual. SPY’s ATR is still elevated enough that daily swings can be meaningful, and extended markets can stay irrational longer than expected. That means tighter discipline, not bigger conviction.

So to be crystal clear: my recommendation is Sell SPY tactically. Not because the long-term bull trend is dead, but because the current price is too extended, too overbought, and too exposed to a normal pullback to justify fresh risk. The better trade is to take some money off the table now and look to re-enter SPY at a better level.
